The infinitude of primes

Kalim Chhoun · 26 December 2024

Number Theory Elementary Proof Euclid

One of the oldest results in mathematics — and still one of the most elegant — is that there are infinitely many prime numbers. The proof below is attributed to Euclid, appearing in Elements around 300 BCE.

Theorem

Theorem

The set of prime numbers is infinite.

Proof

Proof by contradiction

Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there are only finitely many primes. Label them \( p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n \) and define their product:

\[ P = \prod_{i=1}^{n} p_i \]

Now consider the integer \( A = P + 1 \). For each prime \( p_i \) in our list we have:

\[ A \equiv 1 \pmod{p_i} \]

so \( p_i \nmid A \) for every \( i \). Since \( A > 1 \), every integer greater than 1 has at least one prime factor — so \( A \) must have a prime factor not in our original list. This is a contradiction.

\( \therefore \) The set of prime numbers is infinite.

Remark

\( A = P + 1 \) need not itself be prime. The construction only guarantees a new prime factor, not that \( A \) is prime itself.

Several other proofs of this theorem exist. Euler gave an analytic proof using the divergence of \( \sum_{p \text{ prime}} \frac{1}{p} \). Furstenberg (1955) gave a topological proof using arithmetic progressions on \( \mathbb{Z} \).
