Wilson's theorem

Kalim Chhoun · 2 January 2025

Number Theory Elementary Proof Group Theory

For every prime \( p \), the product \( (p-1)! \) is congruent to \( -1 \) modulo \( p \). The proof pairs each element of \( \{1, \ldots, p-1\} \) with its modular inverse, isolating the two self-inverse elements \( 1 \) and \( p-1 \).

Preliminaries

Lemma 1 — Group structure of \( U \)

Let \( p \) be prime and \( U = \{1, 2, \ldots, p-1\} \). Then \( (U, \cdot) \) is an abelian group under multiplication mod \( p \). Every \( u \in U \) has a unique inverse \( u^{-1} \in U \) satisfying \( u \cdot u^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p} \). The group has \( \tfrac{1}{2}(p-1) \) inverse pairs.

Lemma 2 — Self-inverse elements

\( u^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p} \) implies \( p \mid (u-1)(u+1) \). Since \( p \) is prime, either \( p \mid u-1 \) or \( p \mid u+1 \), so \( u \equiv 1 \) or \( u \equiv p-1 \pmod{p} \).

The only self-inverse elements of \( U \) are \( 1 \) and \( p-1 \).

Theorem

Theorem — Wilson's Theorem

For every prime \( p \): \[ (p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \]

Proof

Proof

By Lemma 2, the only self-inverse elements of \( U \) are \( 1 \) and \( p-1 \). All remaining \( \tfrac{1}{2}(p-3) \) elements pair off into distinct pairs \( \{u, u^{-1}\} \) with \( u \neq u^{-1} \), each contributing a product of \( 1 \):

\[ \prod_{\substack{u \in U \\ u \neq 1,\, p-1}} u \;\equiv\; 1 \pmod{p} \]

Therefore:

\[ (p-1)! = 1 \cdot \Bigl(\prod_{\substack{u \in U \\ u \neq 1,\, p-1}} u\Bigr) \cdot (p-1) \equiv 1 \cdot 1 \cdot (p-1) \equiv p-1 \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \]

\( \therefore\ (p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \)

Example: \( p = 5 \)

The inverse pairs in \( U = \{1,2,3,4\} \) are \( \{2,3\} \) since \( 2 \cdot 3 = 6 \equiv 1 \pmod{5} \). The self-inverse elements are \( 1 \) and \( 4 \). Thus:

\[ 4! = (2 \cdot 3) \cdot (1 \cdot 4) \equiv 1 \cdot 4 \equiv 4 \equiv -1 \pmod{5} \]

Remark

Wilson's theorem also gives a characterisation of primes: an integer \( p > 1 \) is prime if and only if \( (p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \). However, computing \( (p-1)! \) is far more expensive than trial division, making this impractical as a primality test.
