Property of co-prime division

Kalim Chhoun · 1 January 2025

Number Theory Elementary Proof Euclid

We prove that if two coprime integers each divide a third integer, then their product divides that integer as well. The proof relies on Euclid's lemma as a key step.

Preliminaries

Lemma — Euclid's Lemma

Let \( a, b, k \in \mathbb{N} \). If \( \gcd(a,b) = 1 \) and \( a \mid kb \), then \( a \mid k \).

Theorem

Theorem — Co-prime Division

\( \forall\, a, b, m \in \mathbb{N} \): if \( a \mid m \) and \( b \mid m \) and \( \gcd(a,b) = 1 \), then \( ab \mid m \).

\[ (a \mid m) \wedge (b \mid m) \wedge \gcd(a,b) = 1 \;\implies\; ab \mid m \]

Proof

Proof

Since \( a \mid m \) and \( b \mid m \), there exist \( k_1, k_2 \in \mathbb{N} \) such that:

\[ k_1 a = m \qquad \text{and} \qquad k_2 b = m \]

Setting these equal gives \( k_1 a = k_2 b \). Since \( a \mid k_2 b \) and \( \gcd(a,b) = 1 \), Euclid's Lemma gives \( a \mid k_2 \). Hence there exists \( j \in \mathbb{N} \) such that \( k_2 = aj \). Substituting back:

\[ m = k_2 b = ajb \]

\( \therefore\ ab \mid m \)

Remark

The condition \( \gcd(a,b) = 1 \) is essential. Take \( a = b = 2 \) and \( m = 6 \): both \( 2 \mid 6 \), yet \( ab = 4 \nmid 6 \). The theorem fails precisely because \( \gcd(2,2) = 2 \neq 1 \).
