Why linear models are almost always useful

Kalim Chhoun · 3 January 2025

Analysis Applied Mathematics Taylor's Theorem

Linear models enjoy a ubiquity in applied mathematics that seems disproportionate to their simplicity. We argue this is mathematically principled: any smooth function is locally linear by Taylor's theorem, and any linearly-growing function is asymptotically equivalent to a linear one.

Setup

Let \( g(x) \) be a function describing some relationship of interest. We ask: under what conditions is it reasonable to model \( g(x) \) as linear, i.e.\ as \( f(x) = cx \)? We answer this in two ways: asymptotically (global behaviour) and locally (behaviour near a point).

Asymptotic equivalence

Definition

Two functions \( f \) and \( g \) are asymptotically equivalent, written \( f(x) \sim g(x) \), if: \[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{g(x)}{f(x)} = C, \qquad C \in \mathbb{R},\ C \neq 0 \]

Proposition

If \( g(x) = ax + b \) is linear with \( a \neq 0 \), then \( g(x) \sim f(x) \) where \( f(x) = x \).

Proof
\[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{g(x)}{f(x)} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{ax + b}{x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \left(a + \frac{b}{x}\right) = a \]

\( \therefore\ g(x) \sim f(x) \)

For non-linear functions, the ratio does not converge to a nonzero constant:

  • Polynomial \( g(x) = x^n,\ n > 1 \): \( f(x) = o(g(x)) \) — linear grows slower
  • Logarithmic \( g(x) = \log x \): \( g(x) = o(f(x)) \) — log grows slower than linear
  • Exponential \( g(x) = e^x \): \( f(x) = o(g(x)) \) — linear grows slower

Local linearity

Theorem — First-order Taylor approximation

Let \( g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \) be differentiable at \( x_0 \). For small \( h \): \[ g(x_0 + h) \approx g(x_0) + g'(x_0)\, h \] The error is \( o(h) \) — it vanishes faster than \( h \) as \( h \to 0 \).

No matter what \( g \) looks like globally, in a sufficiently small neighbourhood of any differentiable point \( x_0 \), it behaves like the linear function \( g(x_0) + g'(x_0)\,h \). This is why linear regression, linearised physics, and linearised control systems work so well in practice.

Conclusion

Linear models are almost always useful for two distinct reasons:

  1. Globally: if \( g(x) \) grows at a linear rate, then \( g(x) \sim cx \), and a linear model captures the dominant behaviour exactly.
  2. Locally: even if \( g \) is not globally linear, Taylor's theorem guarantees a linear approximation with error \( o(h) \) in any sufficiently small neighbourhood.

The ubiquity of linear models is a consequence of two deep mathematical facts about smooth functions — not intellectual laziness.
