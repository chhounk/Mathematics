Decision-making in the context of diversification

Kalim Chhoun · 27 December 2024

Applied Mathematics Finance Frobenius Norm

We propose the Frobenius norm as a tractable criterion for portfolio diversification. Given an existing portfolio represented by a correlation matrix, the optimal asset to add is the one whose inclusion minimises the Frobenius distance between the new correlation matrix and the identity matrix — the theoretical benchmark of perfect diversification.

What is diversification?

Two assets are perfectly diversified with respect to each other if their correlation is zero. A portfolio is perfectly diversified if every pair of distinct assets has zero correlation. Perfect diversification is rarely achievable in practice — the goal is to get as close as possible.

Framework

Let \( P = \{p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\} \) be a portfolio of \( n \) assets. Compute the pairwise correlations of returns and arrange them in a correlation matrix \( C \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \):

\[ C = \begin{bmatrix} r_{11} & r_{12} & \cdots & r_{1n} \\ r_{21} & r_{22} & \cdots & r_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ r_{n1} & r_{n2} & \cdots & r_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \]

where \( r_{ij} \in [-1, 1] \) is the correlation between assets \( p_i \) and \( p_j \), and \( r_{ii} = 1 \) for all \( i \). A perfectly diversified portfolio has \( r_{ij} = 0 \) for all \( i \neq j \), so its correlation matrix reduces to the identity \( I_n \). We take \( I_n \) as the benchmark.

The selection rule

Let \( U = \{u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_m\} \) be candidate assets not yet in the portfolio. To find the best one to add:

01

For each candidate \( u_k \in U \), form the expanded matrix \( C_{u_k} \in \mathbb{R}^{(n+1)\times(n+1)} \) by appending the correlations of \( u_k \) with all existing assets.

02

Compute the deviation from the identity: \( \Delta_{u_k} = C_{u_k} - I_{n+1} \)

03

Compute the Frobenius norm of each deviation matrix:

\[ \|\Delta_{u_k}\|_F = \left( \sum_{i=1}^{n+1}\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \left(\Delta_{u_k}\right)_{ij}^2 \right)^{\!1/2} \]
04

Select the minimiser:

\[ u^* = \arg\min_{u_k \in U} \|\Delta_{u_k}\|_F \]
The asset \( u^* \) brings the portfolio closest to \( I_n \) — closest to perfect diversification.

Closing thoughts

This criterion optimises for diversification, not return. Whether diverse portfolios outperform concentrated ones on a risk-adjusted basis remains an empirical question worth testing.

The model requires only closing price data — universally and freely available. For a first-pass screening tool, this approach is well-suited. More sophisticated models can incorporate ESG factors, liquidity, and investment climate, but at the cost of additional data and modelling assumptions.
