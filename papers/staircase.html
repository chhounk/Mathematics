Counting staircase climbs: a generalised recurrence

Kalim Chhoun · 29 December 2024

Combinatorics Recurrence Relations Fibonacci

How many ways can you climb a staircase of \( m \) steps if you may take between \( 1 \) and \( n \) steps at a time? We derive a general recurrence \( N_{m,n} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} N_{m-i,\,n} \) and show that the Fibonacci and Tribonacci sequences arise as the \( n=2 \) and \( n=3 \) special cases.

Setup

A staircase has \( m \geq 1 \) steps. A climber may take between \( 1 \) and \( n \) steps on each move. Let \( N_{m,n} \) denote the number of distinct ways to climb exactly \( m \) steps. The recurrence requires two boundary conditions:

\[ N_{0,\,n} = 1 \qquad \text{(one way to climb zero steps: do nothing)} \] \[ N_{m,\,n} = 0 \qquad \text{for } m < 0 \]

Theorem

Theorem — Generalised staircase recurrence

Let \( m \geq 1 \) and \( n \geq 1 \). The number of ways to climb \( m \) steps, taking between \( 1 \) and \( n \) steps at a time, satisfies:

\[ N_{m,\,n} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} N_{m-i,\,n} \]

Proof

Proof

Consider the first move. The climber takes exactly \( i \) steps for some \( i \in \{1, \ldots, n\} \). After taking \( i \) steps, exactly \( m - i \) steps remain, and there are \( N_{m-i,\,n} \) ways to complete the climb. Since the first moves are mutually exclusive and exhaustive:

\[ N_{m,\,n} = N_{m-1,\,n} + N_{m-2,\,n} + \cdots + N_{m-n,\,n} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} N_{m-i,\,n} \]

\( \therefore\ N_{m,n} = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} N_{m-i,\,n} \)

Worked example: \( m = 4,\ n = 2 \)

Connection to known sequences

Remark — Fibonacci (\( n = 2 \))

The recurrence \( N_{m,2} = N_{m-1,2} + N_{m-2,2} \) is precisely the Fibonacci recurrence. The sequence is \( 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, \ldots \)

Remark — Tribonacci (\( n = 3 \))

For \( n = 3 \), the recurrence generates the Tribonacci sequence: \( 1, 1, 2, 4, 7, 13, 24, \ldots \) In general \( \{N_{m,n}\} \) is the \( n \)-step Fibonacci sequence.
